The Role of Natural Light in Modern Architecture

By CITRA Associates · March 31, 2025 · 6 min read

Natural light is the oldest and most powerful tool in architecture. Long before electric lighting existed, the quality of a building was determined largely by how well it captured, distributed, and controlled daylight. The best buildings in every architectural tradition — from the light-wells of ancient Indian step wells to the clerestories of medieval cathedrals to the brise-soleil of Le Corbusier — demonstrate an intimate understanding of how natural light can transform space.

In contemporary architecture, natural light matters on multiple dimensions simultaneously: aesthetic, psychological, physiological, and economic. Understanding each of these helps explain why architects treat daylight design as one of their most fundamental responsibilities.

Natural Light and Human Health

The relationship between natural light and human wellbeing is no longer a matter of intuition — it is well-documented science. Natural light regulates the circadian rhythm through specialised photoreceptors in the eye that are sensitive to the blue-spectrum light present in daylight. These receptors suppress melatonin production during daylight hours, keeping us alert and productive, and signal melatonin release in the evening, preparing the body for sleep.

Buildings with inadequate natural light disrupt this cycle. Occupants in naturally dark offices and homes experience higher rates of disturbed sleep, reduced mood, and lower cognitive performance. Studies in schools and offices consistently show that spaces with better natural light produce better outcomes for their occupants — higher test scores in classrooms, higher productivity in workplaces.

In the context of residential design, this means that bedrooms, study spaces, and primary living areas benefit enormously from well-considered daylight design — not just as an aesthetic preference but as a genuine contributor to occupant health and quality of life.

Building Orientation: The Foundation of Daylight Design

No amount of clever window design compensates for incorrect building orientation. Orientation — the compass direction that key facades face — determines the fundamental character of light that enters the building throughout the day and across the year.

For Hyderabad, at approximately 17 degrees north latitude, the following orientations should guide design decisions.

  • North-facing facades: Receive reflected and diffuse sky light throughout the day, never direct sun. This is the most consistent quality of light — ideal for study areas, art rooms, and spaces where glare-free, even illumination is valued.
  • East-facing facades: Receive morning sun that is relatively low-angle and pleasant. Ideal for bedrooms, breakfast areas, and east-facing verandahs.
  • South-facing facades: Receive sun throughout the day in winter (useful) and high-angle summer sun (easily managed with overhangs). This is the most energy-useful orientation for solar-passive strategies.
  • West-facing facades: Receive afternoon sun that is low-angle, hot, and glare-producing. This is the most problematic orientation in Hyderabad's climate and should be minimised or heavily shaded.

Window Design: Size, Position, and Type

Window size is the most common proxy for natural light, but it is a crude one. A single well-positioned window can illuminate a room more effectively than multiple poorly positioned ones. The key variables are window head height, the reflectance of adjacent surfaces, and the relationship between window size and room depth.

Higher window heads distribute light further into a room's depth. Light entering at ceiling level bounces off the ceiling and reaches the middle and back of the room; light entering at desk height illuminates only the immediate vicinity of the window and creates a strong contrast that produces glare.

Clerestory windows — set above normal window height in a raised wall or roof element — are one of the most powerful tools for delivering daylight to the back of deep rooms. They are particularly effective in kitchens, hallways, and living rooms where a standard window on one face would not reach.

Controlling Sunlight: Shading, Overhangs, and Diffusion

The goal of daylight design is not maximum sunlight but optimal quality light. Direct sun entering a space at the wrong time or season creates glare and heat that make the space uncomfortable and increase cooling loads. The architect's job is to admit the light that is wanted and exclude the light that is not.

Overhangs are the most fundamental shading tool. A horizontal overhang sized correctly for the window's latitude will shade the window from high summer sun while allowing low-angle winter sun to penetrate. This is passive solar design in its simplest form — the geometry of the sun's path does the work.

Vertical louvres and fins are more appropriate for east and west windows, where the sun angle is low and horizontal overhangs are insufficient. Adjustable external louvres provide the most flexibility, allowing occupants to control light quality in response to changing conditions.

Translucent materials — frosted glass, polycarbonate, perforated screens — convert harsh direct sunlight into diffuse, ambient light without blocking it entirely. These materials are particularly effective in covered verandahs and transitional spaces where some sun penetration is desired but glare must be managed.

Natural light design is deeply connected to passive cooling strategies for Hyderabad's climate — well-shaded buildings that control solar gain while admitting daylight achieve both thermal comfort and visual quality simultaneously.

Surface Reflectance: How Materials Amplify Daylight

The colour and texture of internal surfaces significantly affect how far natural light penetrates into a space. Light-coloured surfaces — white or near-white walls and ceilings — reflect light deeper into a room, amplifying the effect of windows. Dark surfaces absorb light and reduce its penetration distance dramatically.

This is why kitchens and bathrooms with light-coloured surfaces feel naturally brighter than adjacent rooms of the same window size — not because they have better windows, but because their surfaces are more reflective.

Material specification in a daylight-optimised building considers reflectance systematically. Ceilings are typically the most reflective surface. Walls adjacent to windows are often finished lightly to act as secondary reflectors. Floors receive lower reflectance finishes to reduce upward glare. This creates a graduated reflectance system that the architect manages as part of the material specification process.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does natural light affect the health of building occupants?

Natural light regulates the circadian rhythm — the body's internal clock — by providing the spectrum of light cues that control sleep-wake cycles, hormone production, and mood. Buildings with inadequate natural light are associated with higher rates of seasonal depression, disrupted sleep, and reduced cognitive performance. Adequate daylight in residential and workplace environments is increasingly recognised as a health imperative, not a luxury.

What is the best window orientation for natural light in Hyderabad?

In Hyderabad, north-facing windows provide the most consistent and glare-free natural light throughout the day. East-facing windows give excellent morning light. South-facing windows receive useful winter sun but require overhangs to control summer gains. West-facing windows should be minimised or shaded to avoid harsh afternoon glare and heat gain.

Can too much natural light be a problem in Indian buildings?

Yes. Uncontrolled direct sunlight causes glare, overheats interiors, and increases air conditioning loads. The goal is quality daylight — diffuse, controlled, glare-free natural light that changes character across the day and season. Overhangs, louvres, and translucent screens are the tools architects use to achieve this balance.
